\begin{abstract}
We adapt a 3-qubit channel-entangled Quantum Probability Image Encoding (QPIE) pipeline, originally developed for RGB medical images, to single-lead electrocardiogram (ECG) arrhythmia classification on the PhysioNet/CinC 2017 AF Challenge.
The three colour channels are replaced by three signal channels manufactured from the ECG itself: bandpassed amplitude, first difference, and Hilbert envelope of the 8--16\,Hz wavelet detail.
Six entanglement schemes (Sep, CRyE, GBE, CSE, PA-CSE, CP-2L) encode each $\sim$0.83\,s window into a 3-qubit state. Multi-basis Z+X+Y measurement yields 24 features per window.
We benchmark two pipelines that already exist in our prior work: aggregated MLP and per-window 1D CNN. We then propose QMBC-Net, a Quantum Multi-Basis Cross-attention Network that operates on the un-aggregated $(W \times 24)$ tensor with cross-basis attention over the (Z, X, Y) groups followed by temporal self-attention over windows.
On PhysioNet 2017 the best entangling scheme paired with QMBC-Net reaches macro-F1\,=\,\NUM{XX.XX} on the official N/A/O metric, against \NUM{XX.XX} for the MLP-aggregated baseline and \NUM{XX.XX} for the 1D CNN. Cross-basis attention weights localise the windows and bases that drive each prediction, exposing which cardiac cycles and which projection axes the model relies on.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Atrial fibrillation (AF) is the most common sustained cardiac arrhythmia and a major cause of stroke. Single-lead wearable ECG has made low-cost screening realistic, and the PhysioNet/CinC 2017 AF Challenge~\cite{clifford2017af} put 8528 short single-lead recordings in front of the research community as a public benchmark. Top entries on that challenge sit at macro-F1 around 0.83 over the three clinical classes (Normal, AF, Other), with deeper convolutional and recurrent models pushing this higher on adjacent datasets~\cite{hannun2019cardiologist, andreotti2017comparing}.

Quantum feature extractors are a less explored route. Two questions drive this paper. Does a quantum encoding designed for RGB images transfer to a 1D physiological signal once the three colour channels are replaced by three engineered signal channels? And can a small attention-based head designed around the structure of multi-basis quantum measurements outperform the standard classical heads we have used so far?

We answer both. The encoder family is the six 3-qubit channel-entangled QPIE schemes from our earlier work on MedMNIST. The classical baselines are the same MLP-on-aggregated-features and 1D CNN-on-windows pipelines we have benchmarked before. The new piece is QMBC-Net: a head that splits the 24 multi-basis features per window into three groups of 8 (one per measurement basis), attends across the three basis tokens with a small cross-attention block, then attends across windows with temporal self-attention.

The contributions are three:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{1D adaptation of channel-entangled QPIE.} A reusable recipe for porting a 3-channel RGB quantum encoder to any 1D biosignal: raw amplitude, first difference, and a band-limited envelope as the three channels.
    \item \textbf{QMBC-Net architecture.} A cross-basis $\to$ temporal attention head built specifically for multi-basis quantum features. The basis groups are treated as tokens with their own attention scores, exposing which projection axis carried the signal.
    \item \textbf{Three-tier benchmark on PhysioNet 2017.} Aggregated MLP, per-window 1D CNN, and QMBC-Net evaluated under one protocol against four published baselines, with paired tests across schemes and 5-seed variance reported.
\end{enumerate}

Section~\ref{sec:background} reviews ECG arrhythmia classification and quantum feature extraction. Section~\ref{sec:method} describes the 1D channel construction, the six encoders, and QMBC-Net. Section~\ref{sec:experiments} states the protocol and baselines. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports macro-F1 across the three pipelines and against published results. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses what the basis-attention weights show.

\section{Background and Related Work}
\label{sec:background}

\subsection{ECG arrhythmia classification on PhysioNet 2017}

The 2017 AF Challenge released 8528 single-lead ECG recordings sampled at 300\,Hz, 9 to 60 seconds long, labelled Normal (N), AF (A), Other (O), or Noise ($\sim$). The official metric is macro-F1 over N/A/O. Class balance is roughly 60/9/30/3 percent.

The challenge winner (Clifford et al.) used hand-crafted heart-rate variability and morphology features fed into a gradient-boosted tree, reaching macro-F1\,=\,\NUM{0.83}~\cite{clifford2017af}. Hannun et al.\ on a different and larger single-lead dataset reached F1\,=\,\NUM{0.83} averaged across 12 rhythm classes with a 34-layer 1D ResNet~\cite{hannun2019cardiologist}. Andreotti et al.\ compared feature-engineered and CNN entries on the 2017 challenge and reported macro-F1 between 0.79 and 0.83 across CNN configurations~\cite{andreotti2017comparing}. Goodfellow et al.\ trained a multi-scale CNN on 60-second segments~\cite{goodfellow2018towards}.

\subsection{Quantum image and signal encodings}

Quantum image representations include FRQI~\cite{le2011flexible}, NEQR~\cite{zhang2013neqr}, MCQI~\cite{sun2011multi}, and QPIE~\cite{yao2017quantum}. None directly target temporal signals. Adaptations to time series have used amplitude encoding of the signal itself or angle encoding of fixed-window features~\cite{rahman2024quantum, kyriienko2022unsupervised}.

In a companion paper~\cite{atadana2026qpie} we extended QPIE to RGB by assigning one qubit per channel and varying the entangling structure across six schemes. Multi-basis Z+X+Y measurement and statistical aggregation produced a 492-dimensional feature vector that, paired with a small MLP, classified BloodMNIST and PathMNIST within 0.2 and 9.6 accuracy points of ResNet-18 respectively. The present paper reuses those six encoders and the multi-basis pipeline unchanged.

\subsection{Attention over quantum measurements}

Standard transformer heads treat quantum-feature outputs as plain vectors. The (Z, X, Y) basis structure is then thrown away. We are not aware of a prior architecture that attends across measurement bases as named tokens. The closest design is multi-modal cross-attention, which fuses heterogeneous feature streams~\cite{vaswani2017attention}.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{From single-lead ECG to three signal channels}

A recording $x[n]$ is bandpass-filtered to 0.5--40\,Hz, zero-padded or centre-cropped to 30\,s ($N = 9000$ samples at 300\,Hz), and split into windows of 250 samples ($\sim$0.83\,s, one cardiac cycle) with stride 125. This yields $W = 70$ windows per recording.

For each window we form three channels:
\begin{align}
    c_1[n] &= x_\text{bp}[n] && \text{(raw bandpassed amplitude)} \\
    c_2[n] &= x_\text{bp}[n] - x_\text{bp}[n-1] && \text{(first difference)} \\
    c_3[n] &= |\mathcal{H}\{w_{8\text{--}16}[n]\}| && \text{(envelope of wavelet detail)}
\end{align}
where $w_{8\text{--}16}$ is the wavelet detail in the 8--16\,Hz subband and $\mathcal{H}$ is the Hilbert transform. Each channel is robust-scaled per-recording using the q5/q95 mapping from~\cite{atadana2026qpie} into $[0,1]$.

\subsection{Per-window 3-qubit encoding}

We reuse all six encoders from~\cite{atadana2026qpie}: Sep, CRyE, GBE, CSE, PA-CSE, CP-2L. Each takes a 3-channel sample $(\bar{c}_1, \bar{c}_2, \bar{c}_3) \in [0,1]^3$ at one time step inside a window and returns a 3-qubit amplitude vector. Per-channel angle encoding $\theta_c = \pi \bar{c}_c$ is shared. The schemes differ only in which entangling gates act between the channel qubits. We refer the reader to~\cite{atadana2026qpie} Section III for the full circuit definitions.

For a window of 250 samples we apply the chosen scheme at every sample, project the resulting state onto Z, X, and Y bases (24 features per sample), and aggregate over the 250 samples with the same 11-statistic operator from the prior paper. The output is a fixed $24 \cdot \alpha$-dimensional per-window descriptor (where $\alpha$ depends on the aggregator variant), or, for the un-aggregated path, a $(W \times 24)$ tensor per recording.

\subsection{Three classification heads}

\textbf{Phase-1A: aggregated MLP.} Pooled features over all windows give a 492-dimensional recording-level vector. The MLP is the PathMNIST head from~\cite{atadana2026qpie}: $d \to 512 \to 256 \to 128 \to 4$ with BatchNorm, ReLU, Dropout 0.3.

\textbf{Phase-1B: 1D CNN over windows.} Per-window 24-dim features kept as a $(W \times 24)$ sequence. A three-block 1D CNN with dilations $\{1,2,4\}$, AdaptiveAvgPool1d, then a linear classifier. This is the same CNN1D class used on UWave gestures, where it beat the MLP-on-aggregated path by $\sim$4 accuracy points.

\textbf{Phase-2: QMBC-Net.} For each window, the 24 features are split into three basis tokens of 8 dimensions each, embedded to dimension 64, and passed through a single cross-basis multi-head attention layer (4 heads). A learned per-basis gate weights the three tokens before pooling to a single window vector. Sinusoidal positional encoding is added across the $W$ windows. Two layers of temporal self-attention (4 heads) follow, with a CLS token whose output goes through one Linear layer to the 4 classes.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Dataset and split}

PhysioNet/CinC 2017 AF Challenge~\cite{clifford2017af}, downloaded via \texttt{wfdb}. 8528 recordings, 4 classes. Stratified split 60/20/20 across N/A/O/$\sim$. Five seeds.

\subsection{Training}

All three heads share AdamW (lr $5{\times}10^{-4}$, weight decay $10^{-3}$), cosine annealing, label smoothing 0.05, class-balanced sampler, batch 64, 50 epochs, early stopping on val macro-F1 with patience 10. M4 Mac, MPS backend.

\subsection{Metrics and baselines}

Primary metric: macro-F1 over N/A/O (Noise excluded), the official challenge metric. Secondary: per-class F1, AUC, balanced accuracy. Paired $t$-test across the 5 seeds for the best entangling scheme vs Sep within each pipeline.

Published baselines (see Table~\ref{tab:sota}):
\begin{itemize}
    \item Clifford 2017~\cite{clifford2017af}: hand features + GBT.
    \item Hannun 2019~\cite{hannun2019cardiologist}: 34-layer 1D ResNet (different dataset).
    \item Andreotti 2017~\cite{andreotti2017comparing}: feature vs CNN comparison on the same 2017 challenge.
    \item Goodfellow 2018~\cite{goodfellow2018towards}: multi-scale CNN on 60-s segments.
\end{itemize}
